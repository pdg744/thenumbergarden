# Personas for The Number Garden

This document defines a small, useful set of personas for The Number Garden at its current stage.

The goal is not to invent a large taxonomy. The goal is to help a solo founder make better decisions about:

- who the studio is for
- who actually makes enrollment decisions
- what kinds of support are needed behind the scenes
- which agentic workflows are worth building first

## Stage-aware framing

For now, The Number Garden should think in three layers:

- `Buyer`: usually the parent or caregiver
- `Learner`: the student who experiences the studio directly
- `Advisor`: a human or agent helping the founder make better decisions

In the near term, it is wise to optimize for clarity over coverage. A one-person business cannot serve every plausible segment equally well at launch.

## Primary user personas

These are the most important personas to design around first.

### 1. The Seeking Parent

This is the primary decision-maker.

**Snapshot**

- Parent or caregiver of a child who is not being well served by school math
- Feels that something is off, even if they do not yet have the language for it
- Wants warmth, seriousness, and trustworthiness
- Is not mainly shopping for "test prep"

**What they are trying to solve**

- "My child is capable, but math at school feels deadening."
- "My child used to like math and now avoids it."
- "My child needs challenge, but I do not want pressure and performance culture."
- "I want a place that sees my child as a whole person."

**What they are afraid of**

- Their child being mislabeled as behind, lazy, unmotivated, or not a math person
- Enrolling in something that turns out to be drill-heavy or transactional
- Paying for enrichment that is vague, unserious, or all branding and no substance
- Joining something that is beautiful in theory but chaotic in practice

**What they need from The Number Garden**

- Clear explanation of the philosophy in plain language
- Confidence that rigor is real, even though the tone is warm
- A sense of how fit is assessed
- Signs of safety, structure, and competence
- A founder they can trust personally

**Signals that resonate**

- "Challenge without hurry or coercion"
- "Lower floors, higher ceilings"
- "Rebuilding confidence and fluency"
- "Rich problems worth lingering over"

**What wins them over**

- A thoughtful intake conversation
- Concrete examples of what students actually do
- Language that bridges philosophy and outcomes
- Evidence that you can work with different student starting points without shaming or sorting them

### 2. The Math-Hungry Student

This learner may not be the buyer, but they are often the clearest signal that there is a real fit.

**Snapshot**

- Curious, pattern-seeking, puzzle-loving
- Often underchallenged by school
- May be visibly enthusiastic or quietly intense
- Wants real problems, not busywork

**What they are trying to find**

- A place where math feels alive
- Problems worth thinking about
- Intellectual dignity
- Other people who enjoy real mathematical play

**What frustrates them**

- Repetition without insight
- Being rewarded for speed rather than thought
- Being called "advanced" but offered only more worksheets
- Adult environments that flatten their curiosity

**What they need from The Number Garden**

- Rich tasks with depth
- Freedom to conjecture, test, explain, and revise
- A culture where joy and rigor coexist
- Space to linger instead of always moving on

**What success looks like**

- They want to come back
- They talk about a problem after leaving
- They develop taste, patience, and mathematical language

### 3. The Wounded or Wary Learner

This persona is important because the studio explicitly aims to widen the entrance to mathematics.

**Snapshot**

- Student with uneven confidence, shaky fluency, or a bruised relationship with math
- May seem avoidant, silly, shut down, or convinced they are "bad at math"
- Often carries school-based shame

**What they are trying to recover**

- Safety
- Competence
- A non-humiliating on-ramp back into thinking
- Proof that math can feel different from what they have known

**What they are afraid of**

- Being exposed
- Falling behind in public
- Adults moving too fast
- "Fun math" that is secretly another performance arena

**What they need from The Number Garden**

- Dignity and calm pacing
- Tasks with accessible entry points
- A culture where noticing counts
- Real support for foundations without stigma

**What success looks like**

- They begin to take risks
- They explain an idea out loud
- They stop treating struggle as evidence of incapacity
- They experience genuine mathematical pride

## Secondary user personas

These matter, but they should not drive early product complexity.

### 4. The Values-Aligned Alternative Education Family

- Homeschooling, microschooling, or otherwise looking beyond conventional school structures
- Already receptive to inquiry-based learning
- Likely to understand the philosophy quickly
- Useful early adopters and strong word-of-mouth partners

### 5. The Educator or Collaborator

- Teacher, school leader, tutor, or community-builder
- Interested in the pedagogy, not just the service
- May become a referrer, partner, or future program collaborator

## Near-term recommendation

If we keep the focus tight, the first version of the business should primarily design for:

- The Seeking Parent as buyer
- The Math-Hungry Student as obvious-fit learner
- The Wounded or Wary Learner as values test

That combination fits the current website well. It also prevents the brand from drifting into "gifted-only enrichment" while still preserving the strong challenge signal.

## Advisor personas

These are not customers. These are the roles the business needs in order to make good decisions consistently.

For now, one person can play all of these roles, but it helps to name them separately. These roles also map cleanly to agent support.

### 1. The Pedagogical Steward

This advisor protects the soul of the studio.

**Core question**

- "Does this decision deepen the mathematical culture, or dilute it?"

**Responsibilities**

- Protect the inquiry-driven philosophy
- Curate tasks, rhythms, norms, and room culture
- Keep rigor from collapsing into either entertainment or coercion
- Clarify what fit looks like for different students

**Useful when deciding**

- curriculum
- session structure
- room setup
- behavior norms
- membership design

### 2. The Enrollment Guide

This advisor protects clarity and fit.

**Core question**

- "Can a parent quickly understand whether this is for their child, and what happens next?"

**Responsibilities**

- Shape the intake process
- Translate philosophy into parent-readable language
- Identify objections and friction points
- Improve conversion from curiosity to conversation to enrollment

**Useful when deciding**

- website copy
- inquiry flow
- intake questions
- follow-up emails
- pricing presentation

### 3. The Studio Operator

This advisor protects the founder from chaos.

**Core question**

- "What must be true operationally for this to feel calm, reliable, and sustainable?"

**Responsibilities**

- Capacity planning
- Scheduling
- Policies and communication
- Basic CRM and record-keeping
- Repeatable admin systems

**Useful when deciding**

- class caps
- calendar structure
- cancellation policy
- onboarding steps
- payment flow

### 4. The Trusted Local Builder

This advisor protects community legitimacy.

**Core question**

- "How does this become a real Portland institution rather than just a nice idea online?"

**Responsibilities**

- Build local relationships
- Identify partnerships and early champions
- Notice neighborhood dynamics and access barriers
- Help the studio become known through trust, not just promotion

**Useful when deciding**

- outreach strategy
- local events
- partnerships
- referral channels
- community messaging

### 5. The Founder's Integrator

This advisor protects energy and prioritization.

**Core question**

- "What is the next smallest meaningful move?"

**Responsibilities**

- Turn vision into sequence
- Prevent overbuilding
- Balance pedagogy, sales, operations, and finances
- Keep the founder from fragmenting attention

**Useful when deciding**

- weekly priorities
- launch sequencing
- whether to add a new offer
- what to automate
- what to postpone

## Recommended agentic advisor setup

If the business is a one-man show with agentic support, these are the first advisor agents worth simulating:

### Agent 1. Pedagogy Advisor

Use for:

- evaluating offerings
- refining session design
- reviewing curriculum ideas
- pressure-testing whether messaging matches the real pedagogy

Prompt posture:

- protect rigor
- protect joy
- reject both watered-down fun and sterile advancement culture

### Agent 2. Enrollment and Messaging Advisor

Use for:

- homepage copy
- intake emails
- FAQs
- parent objections
- fit language

Prompt posture:

- translate philosophy into trust
- make next steps obvious
- reduce ambiguity without flattening the vision

### Agent 3. Operator Advisor

Use for:

- turning recurring work into checklists and systems
- documenting workflows
- drafting policies
- spotting bottlenecks before they burn founder energy

Prompt posture:

- prioritize calm systems
- prefer simple repeatable processes
- optimize for founder sustainability, not pseudo-scale

## How to use these personas

When making a decision, ask four quick questions:

1. Which persona is this for?
2. Is that persona a buyer, learner, or advisor?
3. What fear or desire are we addressing?
4. Does this make the studio clearer, warmer, and more trustworthy?

If a decision cannot be tied to a clear persona, it is probably not urgent.

## Practical filters for the next phase

Over the next few weeks, these personas suggest a few healthy constraints:

- Write primarily to the Seeking Parent, but always preserve dignity for the Learner.
- Show enough specificity that serious families can recognize real pedagogy.
- Do not let "enrichment" become code for "only for already-confident kids."
- Build the intake process before building too many offerings.
- Use agents mainly to support pedagogy, messaging, and operations, not to create fake breadth.

## Working assumptions

These personas are based on the current site and may need revision once real inquiries start coming in.

Assumptions made here:

- The immediate market is local families in Portland
- Parents are the primary economic decision-makers
- The first business challenge is trust and fit, not mass awareness
- The studio wants to serve both mathematically eager students and students rebuilding their relationship with math

Once you have 10 to 20 real inquiries, this document should be revised using actual patterns in:

- how families describe the problem
- what objections arise
- which offerings generate the most interest
- which students actually thrive in the space
